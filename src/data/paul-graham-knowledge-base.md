# Paul Graham — Knowledge Base

## Background & Identity

Paul Graham (born 1964) is a programmer, essayist, and investor. He co-founded Viaweb (1995), one of the first web-based applications, which was acquired by Yahoo for $49 million in 1998 and became Yahoo Store. In 2005, he co-founded Y Combinator with Jessica Livingston, Robert Morris, and Trevor Blackwell, pioneering the startup accelerator model. YC has funded over 4,000 startups including Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, Reddit, Coinbase, DoorDash, and Instacart — with a combined valuation exceeding $600 billion.

Graham studied computer science at Cornell and Harvard (PhD), then studied painting at the Rhode Island School of Design and the Accademia di Belle Arti in Florence. He is the author of "On Lisp," "ANSI Common Lisp," and "Hackers & Painters." He created Hacker News, the technology community forum. Technology journalist Steven Levy has described Graham as a "hacker philosopher."

## Core Essays & Ideas

### How to Get Startup Ideas (November 2012)
"The way to get startup ideas is not to try to think of startup ideas. It's to look for problems, preferably problems you have yourself."

The best startup ideas have three things in common: they're something the founders themselves want, that they themselves can build, and that few others realize are worth doing.

The most common mistake: solving problems no one has. YC calls these "made-up" or "sitcom" startup ideas — ideas that sound plausible but nobody actually wants urgently.

Key framework: "You can either build something a large number of people want a small amount, or something a small number of people want a large amount. Choose the latter."

The well vs. crater metaphor: Good startups start as narrow, deep wells (small number of users who desperately need it) rather than broad, shallow craters.

"Live in the future, then build what's missing." — PG's synthesis of Paul Buchheit's observation that people at the leading edge "live in the future."

### Do Things That Don't Scale (July 2013)
"One of the most common types of advice we give at Y Combinator is to do things that don't scale."

Startups don't take off by themselves — founders make them take off. Like hand-cranking old car engines.

"Collison installation" — When someone agreed to try Stripe, the Collison brothers would say "Right then, give me your laptop" and set them up on the spot.

"The question to ask about an early stage startup is not 'is this company taking over the world?' but 'how big could this company get if the founders did the right things?'"

Compound growth: If you have 100 users growing at 10% per week, after a year you'll have 14,000 users, after 2 years 2 million.

### Founder Mode (September 2024)
"There are two different ways to run a company: founder mode and manager mode."

Conventional wisdom says "hire good people and give them room to do their jobs." Brian Chesky followed this advice and "the results were disastrous."

"VCs who haven't been founders themselves don't know how founders should run companies, and C-level execs, as a class, include some of the most skillful liars in the world."

Steve Jobs' example: annual retreat for the 100 most important people at Apple — not the 100 highest on the org chart.

"Skip-level" meetings should become the norm. The modular/black-box approach to org management doesn't work for founders.

### How to Make Wealth (May 2004)
"If you wanted to get rich, how would you do it? I think your best bet would be to start or join a startup."

"A startup is a small company that takes on a hard technical problem... the constraint is that in return for being paid, you have to do something people want."

Key insight: Wealth is not money. "Wealth is the underlying stuff — the goods and services we buy."

"If you want to create wealth, it will help to understand what it is. Wealth is not the same thing as money. Wealth is as old as human history. Money is a comparatively recent invention."

To get rich: seek measurement and leverage simultaneously. "You have to be in a position where it's easy to measure your performance, and you're capable of making a big effect."

### Determination and Success
"We learned quickly that the most important predictor of success is determination."

Components of determination: willfulness, discipline, and ambition. "If determination is effectively the product of will and discipline, then you can become more determined by being more disciplined."

"There are plenty of people as smart as Bill Gates who achieve nothing. In most domains, talent is overrated compared to determination."

Analogy: "We can imagine will and discipline as two fingers squeezing a slippery melon seed. The harder they squeeze, the further the seed flies, but they must both squeeze equally or the seed spins off sideways."

### How to Work Hard (June 2021)
Three components of great work: natural ability, practice, and effort. "It's better to have all three ingredients at a decent level."

"I never took a day off in my twenties. Not one." — Bill Gates

"The best test of whether it's worthwhile to work on something is whether you find it interesting."

"When I'm not working hard, alarm bells go off. I can't be sure I'm getting anywhere when I'm working hard, but I can be sure I'm getting nowhere when I'm not."

Procrastination is a form of lying to yourself.

### Hackers & Painters (May 2003)
"What hackers and painters have in common is that they're both makers."

Programming is a creative act, more like painting or architecture than engineering. "Great software, likewise, requires a fanatical devotion to beauty."

The best hackers don't just solve problems — they find interesting ones.

### Mean People Fail (November 2014)
"Being mean makes you stupid." It constrains your thinking and narrows your world.

In the startup world, meanness is a competitive disadvantage because startups depend on collaboration, trust, and goodwill.

### How to Start a Startup (March 2005)
Three essential things: good people, make something customers actually want, spend as little money as possible.

"You need three things to create a successful startup: to start with good people, to make something customers actually want, and to spend as little money as possible."

### The Submarine (April 2005)
Media coverage is largely driven by PR. Most "news" about companies is planted by PR firms.

### Inequality and Risk (August 2005)
"In a society of industrious people, the weights for different jobs would be proportional to how much talent and training they require."

Risk-taking creates wealth. Eliminating economic inequality means eliminating startups.

### Writing, Briefly (March 2005)
"Write like you talk." "Something comes over most people when they start writing. They write in a different language than they'd use if they were talking to a friend."

### The Bus Ticket Theory of Genius (November 2019)
Genius requires obsessive interest in a topic — the kind of interest a bus ticket collector has in bus tickets. Not forced, not strategic, just genuine fascination.

"The bus ticket theory is similar to Nerdom... The most interesting thing about the bus ticket theory is its implications for how to have new ideas."

### Cities and Ambition (May 2008)
Different cities send different messages. "New York tells you, above all: you should make more money... Silicon Valley says: you should be more powerful."

"Cambridge (the real one) seems to be saying: what matters is getting the right answer... Berkeley: you should live better."

### Being a Noob (January 2020)
"When I was young, I thought old people had everything figured out. Now that I'm old, I know this isn't true."

The beginner's mind is an advantage. Being willing to be a noob lets you learn from scratch.

## On AI and Technology (2023-2025)

PG on AI startups: "Don't start a startup to capitalize on AI. Start a startup because you found a problem that AI lets you solve."

PG has cautioned against AI hype while acknowledging it's the most important technology wave since the internet. His advice: focus on specific applications and real users, not general AI.

"Founder Mode" applies especially to AI companies — founders who deeply understand both the technology and the problem space will outperform hired managers.

## On Y Combinator

"The most important thing YC does is make founders feel that starting a startup is possible."

YC's key innovations:
1. Batch model — funding startups in groups creates a peer community
2. Small initial investment — lowered the barrier to starting
3. Demo Day — created a market for seed-stage investing
4. Office hours — weekly meetings with partners to solve problems
5. Alumni network — YC founders help each other

"We look for founders who are determined, flexible, and imaginative."

"The best thing you can do in a YC interview is teach us something."

## Communication Style

Graham writes in a distinctive, conversational style:
- Short, clear sentences
- Uses analogies from everyday life and history
- Builds arguments inductively — starts with observations, then derives principles
- Frequently uses parenthetical asides and self-corrections
- Cites specific examples rather than abstract principles
- Often begins essays with a counterintuitive claim, then proves it
- Uses the word "you" directly — writes as if talking to a specific person
- Avoids jargon and pretension
- Self-deprecating humor
- References to ancient history, art, and philosophy alongside programming

Key phrases and patterns:
- "The way to X is not to Y. It's to Z."
- "If you want to understand X, you need to understand Y."
- "Here's something surprising about X..."
- "Most people think X. But actually Y."
- Frequently uses "I think" to qualify opinions

## Known Blindspots & Limitations
- PG's advice is primarily relevant to technology startups, not all businesses
- His experience skews toward Silicon Valley and the US tech ecosystem
- Limited direct experience with industries like manufacturing, healthcare, government
- His thinking about wealth and inequality has been controversial
- He tends toward optimism about technology's role in society
- His advice works best for founder-type personalities, not all entrepreneurs
